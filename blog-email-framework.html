Email Design

The Framework Behind 20+ High-Converting Email Templates

February 20265 min readOlaitan Hammed

After designing and coding 20+ production email templates for e-commerce and SaaS brands, I've settled on a framework that consistently drives clicks and revenue. It's not a template — it's a structural system that works across industries, audiences, and campaign types.

Here's the system, open-sourced from my design process.

The Three Pillars

01

Hero-First

Lead with the strongest visual and message. The first scroll is everything.

02

Visual-First

Images sell. Typography supports. White space breathes. Ratio: 60/40 visual to text.

03

CTA-Repeat

Place the primary CTA at least 3 times. Top, middle, bottom. Every scroll depth gets an exit.

Pillar 1: Hero-First

The hero section is the most critical piece of any email. 80% of recipients decide whether to keep scrolling based on the first 300 pixels. My hero sections always include:

  • A full-width visual — product photography, lifestyle shot, or branded illustration
  • One headline — 6 words or fewer, benefit-driven, not feature-driven
  • One CTA button — high contrast, action-oriented ("Shop Now", "Get Started", "Claim Your Spot")

No navigation bars, no social icons, no secondary links. The hero has one job: get the click or get the scroll.

Pillar 2: Visual-First

Email isn't a blog post. The moment an email feels like "reading," you've lost half your audience. My templates maintain a 60/40 ratio of visual content to text.

This means:

  • Product grids instead of product descriptions
  • Infographic-style benefit blocks instead of bullet lists
  • Icon + short label instead of paragraphs
  • Lifestyle imagery that shows the product in context

Every visual element earns its space. If an image doesn't move the reader toward clicking, it's removed.

Pillar 3: CTA-Repeat

One CTA button is a mistake. People scroll at different speeds and stop at different points. My rule: place the primary CTA at minimum 3 times throughout the email.

  1. Hero CTA — Catches the instant-action readers (top 20%)
  2. Mid-content CTA — After the main value proposition or product showcase
  3. Footer CTA — For readers who scrolled the entire email and are now convinced

The CTA button text changes at each position. Hero says "Shop Now." Middle says "See the Collection." Footer says "Don't Miss Out." Same destination, different micro-motivation.

The Technical Layer

A beautiful design that breaks in Outlook is worthless. Every template I build follows these technical rules:

  • Table-based layout — Because email clients aren't browsers. Flexbox and grid don't work.
  • Inline CSS only — Most email clients strip <style> blocks. Every style is inline.
  • 600px max width — The universal safe width across all email clients
  • Responsive breakpoints — Media queries for clients that support them (Apple Mail, iOS, Android)
  • Dark mode support — Color swaps and image alternatives for dark mode rendering
  • Alt text on every image — For clients that block images by default (Outlook, corporate)
  • Fallback fonts — Web fonts with system font fallbacks (Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif)

Email Types & How the Framework Adapts

Product Launch

Hero dominates (50% of email height). Single product focus. Urgency language ("Just Dropped", "Limited Run"). 4+ CTAs.

Promotional / Sale

Discount value front and center. Countdown timer if possible. Product grid with prices. Coupon code in a styled box that's easy to copy.

Welcome / Onboarding

Warm tone. Brand story + values. Discount incentive for first purchase. 3-step "what to do next" section. Social proof (reviews, user count).

Retention / Re-engagement

"We miss you" angle. Personalized product recommendations. Exclusive offer. Simple, clean — no information overload.
